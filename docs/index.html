Abstract
Around 43% of worlds population does not have access to safe sanitation. In my PhD I will research a new approach to treat faeces using filamentous fungi. The goal of the research is to find fungal species that grow on human faeces and help reduce concentrations of faecal indicator bacteria. To be able to compare the growth speed and antibacterial effect of different fungal species a data analysis is necessary. With this report the way to manage and analyse the data was created.

Exploring fungi for faecal treatment

Introduction

Globally, 43% of the population lacks access to safe sanitation (Population Using Safely Managed Sanitation Services (, n.d.), leading to the spread of diarrheal diseases and environmental contamination from untreated faecal sludge. While urine diversion dry toilets (UDDTs) offer a potential solution by separating nutrient-rich and safe urine for fertilizer, the treatment of faeces is still a challenge. Long storage times and potential pathogen reactivation in dried faeces remain can be a risk for spreading of disease(Kumwenda et al., 2017).

The introduction of filamentous fungi, to sanitize faeces is a novel approach yet to be studied. Fungi are powerful decomposers in nature, utilizing enzymes to break down complex organic matter (Berg & McClaugherty, 2020). Coprophilous fungi, adapted to nutrient-rich dung, have evolved mechanisms to thrive in environments with high microbial competition, they are able to sense bacteria and produce antibacterial compounds up on recognition (Kombrink et al., 2019). Interestingly, preliminary research indicates that even non-dung-adapted white-rot fungi can colonize human faeces and reduce odour.

This doctoral thesis aims to investigate the potential of fungal species to utilize faeces as a nutrient source, reduce the concentration of harmful bacteria, and alter the physical properties of the faecal mass. Understanding these processes could lead to the development of an effective and sustainable method for treating faecal waste in UDDTs, mitigating health risks and shortening the required storage time for safe disposal or agricultural use.

Within this lecture of Research beyond the lab (RBTL) the code for data cleaning and analysis will be developed and tested using a example data set with simulated fungal growth and bacteria data. In order to test the capability of fungi to grow on human faeces, a petri dish based assay will be developed to measure growth speed and log-change of E. coli and Enterococcus before and after fungal over growth.

The Shapiro-Wilk test verifies the normal distribution for C. comatus and faecal isolate 1 while it shows violation of normal distribution for P. ostreatus v. Floridae Table 1. Figure 3 shows the median of the mean growth speed and distribution of the different fungal species in a Box-plot. As the mean growth speed is not normally distributed, a non-parametric test like the Kruskal-Wallis test would usually be recommended. But with the prepared script we want to be able to test both options. To test for differences in the mean growth among the different fungi with normally distributed growth speed values, an analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted on the dataset. The ANOVA Table 2 followed by a Tukey post-hoc analysis Table 3 indicated no significant difference in the mean growth speed between any of the species pairs

###ANOVA for growth speed between different species

To complete the test setting the data will also be analysed using a non parametric test. In this case a Kruskal-Wallis test Table 4 and a Dunn post-hoc analysis Table 5 are done

# Make sure 'species' is a factor
growth_speed_analysis <- growth_speed_analysis |>
  mutate(species = as.factor(species))

# Perform the Kruskal-Wallis test using rstatix
kruskal_result_growth_speed <- growth_speed_analysis |>
  kruskal_test(mean_growth_speed ~ species)


 kruskal_growth_speed_table <- kruskal_result_growth_speed|>
  select(-.y.) %>% # Remove the '.y.' column for cleaner output
  gt()
  
kruskal_growth_speed_table  
dunn_result <- growth_speed_analysis |>
  dunn_test(mean_growth_speed ~ species, p.adjust.method = "bonferroni")|>
  gt()

# Print the Dunn's test results
dunn_result

Neither of the used tests under assuming normal distribution or rejecting normal distribution showed significant results between the growth speed of the fungal species in this simulated data set.

Analysis of bacterial concentration changes

In this section, the bacterial log-change for E. coli and E. faecalis will be analyzed. This log-change is calculated from the initial bacterial concentration measured in the fecal samples and the concentration measured after co-incubation, indicating the log10​ reduction in bacterial concentration. This normalization is necessary due to varying initial bacterial concentrations in the starting substrate. To test the collected data for normal distribution, a Q-Q plot and a Shapiro-Wilk test is used.

ggplot(ecoli_log, aes(sample = `log_change_ecoli`)) +
  geom_qq() +
  facet_wrap(vars( species)) +

The Shapiro test Table 6 and the Q-Q plots Figure 4 showing that the created log change data for E. coli and E. faecalis Table 7 and Figure 5 is normal distributed. This normal distribution allows to use a parametric test. To analyse bacterial log change an ANOVA was used and a Tukey post-hoc analysis to test between different treatment pairs.

Within the generated dataset, statistically significant differences (p<0.05) in the log-change of both E. coli and E. faecalis were observed across the experimental groups Table 8.

A subsequent Tukey post-hoc analysis for E. coli Table 9) indicated that fungal interventions utilizing P. ostreatus and C. comatus produced significantly distinct log-change values when compared to the control and fecal isolate 1. In contrast, no significant difference was detected between the control and fecal isolate 1.

For E. faecalis, the treatment involving P. ostreatus demonstrated a significantly greater log-change in bacterial concentration relative to all other treatments, with no significant differences found among the remaining experimental groups Table 11.

Conclusions

The developed data structure and scripts can be applied to the test dataset to visualize differences in mean growth speed among species and the log-change in bacterial concentrations before and after fungal growth. For statistical analysis, ANOVA and Tukey’s post-hoc test are suitable for normally distributed data, while the Kruskal-Wallis and Dunn’s tests are appropriate for non-normally distributed data. Applying these scripts to the actual collected data may require some adaptations, primarily due to necessary modifications in the assay methodology.
